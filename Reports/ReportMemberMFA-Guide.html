1. How to read this report

The report answers one question for every member account in the tenant:

“If this person tries to sign in — under any combination of device, network, application, location, or risk — are they covered by MFA as much as our Conditional Access policies can guarantee?”

1.1 Personas the report deals with

  • Internal — a Member account inside this Microsoft 365 tenant. Regular staff. This is who the cards count.
  • Admins — a Member account that currently holds an active Entra ID admin role. They are still members, but their auth methods often cannot be read by the script (see “Access Denied” in Section 3).
  • Guests — external user accounts (B2B). Excluded from this report entirely — they sit under a different control set.

1.2 Three groups of cards, in the order they appear

  1. Accounts — how many member accounts exist and which are alive.
  2. Auth Methods — for the live accounts, what kind of MFA each user has registered (Axis 1).
  3. MFA Posture — combines Axis 1 with Axis 2 (CA coverage) to produce a verdict per user.

4. The CA Policy Framework — Ideal, Acceptable, Ignored

Before we can talk about the third group of cards, we need to talk about Conditional Access (CA) policies. The framework looks at every MFA-enforcing CA policy in the tenant once and assigns it to a tier. This happens independently of any user.

In Scoring v1.0, only Ideal policies count towards user coverage. The other tiers are listed in the CA Policies table on the full report for transparency, but they do not move the posture numbers. This is deliberate — the framework's goal is to start from a baseline we can prove, then expand in later versions.

4.1 Ideal — counts toward coverage

An Ideal policy enforces MFA in a way we can rely on across every sign-in scenario for the users it targets. To earn Ideal in v1.0, a policy must satisfy all of the following:

  • State is Enabled (not Report-only, not Off).
  • The grant control requires MFA — either directly (mfa) or via an MFA-capable Authentication Strength.
  • If multiple grant controls are listed, they are joined with AND, not OR. (An OR with a non-MFA companion control means the user can sign in without doing MFA.)
  • It fires unconditionally — it is not gated by sign-in risk or user risk. (Risk-based policies are valuable, but they only kick in some of the time.)
  • The audience is a broad user scope — All users or a baseline group covering all Internal members. Admins-only and Guests-only policies are out of scope here; they are scored separately.
  • No narrowing conditions are present. Specifically: no conditional exclusions (e.g. excluding an office IP location), and no explicit inclusions that act as filters (e.g. ticking specific Device Platforms, Client Apps, or named locations).
Why “no narrowing conditions” matters. Conditions such as “apply only to Windows and macOS” or “exclude trusted office IPs” create gaps a determined attacker can drive a truck through. For example, a policy that targets only specific Device Platforms can be bypassed by a client that reports an unrecognised platform — Conditional Access doesn't evaluate the policy at all, and the user signs in with just a password. The same logic applies to client-app filters and location exclusions: any control point an attacker can spoof or move around weakens the guarantee. In v1.0 we don't try to model which conditions are “safe” — we just require there are none, so the result is honest.

4.2 Acceptable — visible, not counted in v1.0

An Acceptable policy enforces MFA but uses narrowing conditions that mean we cannot claim universal coverage from it — the very things called out in 4.1 above. Typical examples:

  • Targets only specific Device Platforms or Client App types.
  • Excludes named locations such as office IPs.
  • Scoped to a single application instead of All cloud apps.
  • Scoped to a specific department or team rather than a baseline group.

These policies probably are doing real work in the tenant. The framework just cannot assume the work is universal, so v1.0 does not credit them in the coverage maths. A future scoring version will revisit them with finer-grained logic.

4.3 Ignored — visible, deliberately discarded

The framework cannot trust an Ignored policy to enforce MFA at all. Examples:

  • Risk-based only — fires only when Entra ID detects elevated risk.
  • Grant control uses OR with a non-MFA companion control — MFA can be bypassed.
  • The user can satisfy the grant control with something other than MFA (e.g. compliant device only).
  • Targets Admins only — doesn't apply to regular members.
  • Targets Guests only — guests are out of scope for this report.
“Ignored” does not mean “useless”. Risk-based and admin policies are valuable security controls. “Ignored” in this framework only means we will not count them as evidence that a regular Internal member is MFA-protected on every sign-in.

4.4 What this means for the MFA Posture cards

When the report decides whether a user has Full CA coverage in v1.0, it only consults Ideal policies. If the tenant has zero Ideal policies, every user is recorded as having None coverage — regardless of how many Acceptable or Ignored policies exist.

This is intentional for v1.0. CA policy logic is complex and exhaustively modelling every combination of conditions is a much larger piece of work. We have started from the ideal state because that's where we can produce a result that is both meaningful and impactful — an answer the report can prove rather than estimate.

5. CA Coverage (Axis 2)

For each enabled member, the report asks: do any Ideal policies actually apply to this person? The answer is one of three values. This is the second half of the MFA Posture calculation.

BucketWhat it meansHow we decide (v1.0)
Full At least one Ideal policy exists and the user is on none of its exclusion lists. Tenant has ≥ 1 Ideal policy and the user does not appear on any Ideal policy's ExcludeUsers, ExcludeGroups (resolved transitively), or ExcludeRoles.
Partial Ideal policies exist, but this user has been excluded from at least one. The user's CaExclusionTags field on the full report lists the IDs of the Ideal policies that exclude them.
None The tenant has zero Ideal policies. No coverage can be claimed for anyone. No CA policy in the tenant satisfies the Ideal criteria in Section 4.1.

8.1 Frequently asked questions

Q1
The tenant has a working MFA policy — why does the report still show Unenforced or Critical?
A1
Because that policy did not qualify as Ideal in v1.0. Open the CA Policies table on the full report and look at the policy's tier badge. The Conditions and Workloads columns usually show why — commonly: risk-based, narrow audience, narrow Device Platforms, or an excluded named location.
Q2
Why does an admin show as Unknown / Access Denied?
A2
Reading authentication methods for a privileged account requires elevated Graph permissions that the running identity may not hold. It is a permissions question, not an MFA gap.
